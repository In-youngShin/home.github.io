About Me

Hi, I’m a data scientist and analyst with a unique blend of expertise in data engineering, advanced analytics, and system documentation. My work bridges social science and computer science, enabling me to translate complex human behaviors into actionable, data-driven solutions. I specialize in requirements gathering, data integration, exploratory data analysis (EDA), and predictive modeling—leveraging Python, R, SQL, and API integration.

I have led end-to-end data projects involving survey, behavioral, and multi-source datasets, applied statistical modeling (regression, mixed-effects models), and built machine learning workflows using PyTorch. My experience includes producing clear technical documentation, designing testing workflows, and communicating results to diverse stakeholders.

I hold a Ph.D. in Communication from Rutgers University and an M.S. in Computer Science from Yale University. My work has been published in peer-reviewed journals, cited over 1,000 times on Google Scholar and featured in collaborations with organizations like the Pew Research Center.

Apply rigorous data science methods to solve complex problems, my dream is to make technology more human-aware—and more useful because of it.

Applied Projects

1) Human-AI and Robot Interaction

I examine how people perceive and interact with AI systems and robots, particularly in decision-making contexts where fairness, trust, and accountability are critical. My work explores how changes in a robot’s perceived agency affect user evaluations—such as fairness judgments, blame, and moral trust—and how these perceptions evolve over time. This research has involved requirements gathering, experiment design, building and maintaining data pipelines, Python-based automation, large language model (LLM) applications for qualitative data analysis, and documentation aligned with agile and waterfall concepts.

    Selected Projects/Publications
  • Claure, H., Shin, I.*, Trafton, G., & Vázquez, M. (2025). Did the Robot Really Intend to Harm Me? The Effect of Perceived Agency and Intention on Fairness Judgments ACM/IEEE HRI. *Equal contribution.
  • Shin, I., Choung, H., & Choi, M. (under review). Does AI narrow skill gaps? AI‑assisted professional writing by non‑native and native English writers.
  • Shin, I., Claure, H., Blaustein, M., Trafton, G., & Vázquez, M. (in prep.). Effects of time‑varying perceived agency on fairness judgments toward robots.

2) Social Impacts of Digital Media

This line of projects examines how digital technology adoption influences collaboration, knowledge-sharing, and network growth. The work involves defining measurable outcomes, testing hypotheses with statistical models, and integrating qualitative insights.
